How do the operator-theoretic properties of population models change when their biological inputs are constrained by metabolic scaling laws?

MS Project: MTE-IPM

Mathematical Biology Operator Theory Perturbation Theory

The Integral Projection Model (IPM) is a framework for continuously size-structured populations. The population density $n(m, t)$ by body mass evolves as

n(m, t+1) = ∫ 𝒦(m, m') n(m', t) dm'

where the kernel $\mathcal{K} = P + F$ combines survival-growth and fecundity. The dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ of the operator $\mathcal{K}$ governs asymptotic population growth — if $\lambda_1 > 1$ the population is supercritical; if $\lambda_1 < 1$ extinction is certain.

The Metabolic Theory of Ecology (MTE, Brown et al. 2004) provides a principled derivation of vital rates from body mass $m$ and temperature $T$:

growth rate ∝ m¼ · e−E/kT
mortality ∝ m−¼ · e−E/kT
fecundity ∝ m¾ · e−E/kT

When MTE vital rates are substituted into the IPM kernel, the operator $\mathcal{K}$ inherits specific algebraic structure from metabolic scaling. Our project asks: what does this structure imply for the spectral properties of $\mathcal{K}$, and how does $\lambda_1$ behave under perturbation by temperature shifts or resource fluctuations?

Mathematical approach

The formal framework comes from Kato's Perturbation Theory for Linear Operators. The key ingredients we need: the resolvent $R(\zeta) = (\mathcal{K} - \zeta)^{-1}$ as an analytic operator-valued function; the eigenprojection $P_1$ onto the dominant eigenspace via contour integration; and the analytic perturbation theory of eigenvalues for the temperature-parameterised family $\mathcal{K}(T)$.

A central open question is whether the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E/kT}$ — which is real-analytic in $T$ — makes $\mathcal{K}(T)$ an analytic family of compact operators in the sense of Kato Ch. VII. If so, $\lambda_1(T)$ would be analytic in temperature, giving a clean functional form for how extinction risk varies with warming.

Background and reading

Kato — Perturbation Theory for Linear Operators
Primary text · reading ongoing
Building the operator-theoretic foundations from Ch. I (finite-dimensional) through Ch. II–III (bounded operators on Banach spaces) toward Ch. VII (analytic perturbation theory). Weekly reading logs on the notes page.
Rudin — Real and Complex Analysis
Foundation · Measure Theory course
The measure-theoretic language underlying integration in infinite-dimensional function spaces — $L^2$, Hilbert-Schmidt operators, spectral measures. Essential for carrying IPM integrals into a rigorous analytic framework.
Brown et al. (2004) — Toward a metabolic theory of ecology
MTE foundational paper · Ecology 85(7)
The source of the metabolic scaling laws the project imports into the IPM kernel. The mathematical formalism question begins here: these are empirical scaling laws — what operator-theoretic properties do they imply?

Earlier interests

Before settling on the MTE-IPM direction, my reading arc went: theoretical biology papers → frustration at informal reasoning → stochastic processes (self-studied, summer 2024) → measure theory → operator theory. The thread was always: what is the right mathematical language for biological dynamics?

I remain interested in stochastic population models, particularly how demographic stochasticity enters the IPM framework and whether mean-time-to-extinction results can be obtained analytically from the spectral data.
