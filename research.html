This MS thesis develops a rigorous operator-theoretic framework for the Metabolic Inte- gral Projection Model (MIPM), a class of continuous-state population models in math- ematical ecology, with the primary contribution directed squarely at functional analysis and spectral theory. The central object of study is the integral operator K : C(Ω) → C(Ω) defined by (Kφ)(z ′ ) = Z Ω K(z ′ , z; T)φ(z) dz, (1) where Ω ⊂ R+ is a compact size domain and T a real parameter; the first objective is to prove compactness of K via the Arzel`a–Ascoli theorem, establishing that the spectrum of K admits a Riesz–Schauder decomposition and may be analysed by the same tools as finite-dimensional matrices. The second objective applies the Krein–Rutman theorem — the infinite-dimensional generalisation of the Perron–Frobenius theorem — to an ordered Banach space setting, yielding a complete proof of the existence, simplicity, and strict positivity of the spectral radius r(K) as a dominant eigenvalue with a corresponding pos- itive eigenfunction; this constitutes a self-contained contribution to the theory of positive operators on ordered Banach spaces. The third and mathematically deepest objective ap- plies Kato’s analytic perturbation theory for linear operators to the parameterised family {K(T)}T ∈I , deriving the first-order expansion dλ1 dT = ⟨ψ1, K′ (T)φ1⟩ ⟨ψ1, φ1⟩ (2) via resolvent calculus, with full attention to the analytic dependence of spectral projec- tions on the parameter T; the ecological motivation for this formula — quantifying how a population growth rate responds to thermal perturbation — serves only as the applied context, while the mathematical content is a concrete instantiation of Kato’s theory for a class of integral operators not previously treated in this generality. The thesis will be conducted under the supervision of Dr. Samraat Pawar (Imperial College London), with me spending five months on-site at Imperial, and will be written to the standard of mathematical exposition expected at IISER Berhampur, with all biological quanti- ties re-derived from first principles as corollaries of operator-theoretic results — making this, in character and in contribution, a thesis in applied functional analysis<

The formal framework comes from Kato's Perturbation Theory for Linear Operators. The key ingredients we need: the resolvent $R(\zeta) = (\mathcal{K} - \zeta)^{-1}$ as an analytic operator-valued function; the eigenprojection $P_1$ onto the dominant eigenspace via contour integration; and the analytic perturbation theory of eigenvalues for the temperature-parameterised family $\mathcal{K}(T)$.

A central open question is whether the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E/kT}$ — which is real-analytic in $T$ — makes $\mathcal{K}(T)$ an analytic family of compact operators in the sense of Kato Ch. VII. If so, $\lambda_1(T)$ would be analytic in temperature, giving a clean functional form for how extinction risk varies with warming.

Background and reading

Kato — Perturbation Theory for Linear Operators
Primary text · reading ongoing
Building the operator-theoretic foundations from Ch. I (finite-dimensional) through Ch. II–III (bounded operators on Banach spaces) toward Ch. VII (analytic perturbation theory). Weekly reading logs on the notes page.
Rudin — Real and Complex Analysis
Foundation · Measure Theory course
The measure-theoretic language underlying integration in infinite-dimensional function spaces — $L^2$, Hilbert-Schmidt operators, spectral measures. Essential for carrying IPM integrals into a rigorous analytic framework.
Brown et al. (2004) — Toward a metabolic theory of ecology
MTE foundational paper · Ecology 85(7)
The source of the metabolic scaling laws the project imports into the IPM kernel. The mathematical formalism question begins here: these are empirical scaling laws — what operator-theoretic properties do they imply?

Earlier interests

Before settling on the MTE-IPM direction, my reading arc went: theoretical biology papers → frustration at informal reasoning → stochastic processes (self-studied, summer 2024) → measure theory → operator theory. The thread was always: what is the right mathematical language for biological dynamics?

I remain interested in stochastic population models, particularly how demographic stochasticity enters the IPM framework and whether mean-time-to-extinction results can be obtained analytically from the spectral data.
